Problem and Solution

Problem Statement

Two particles, 1 and 2, move with constant velocities $\mathbf{v}_1$ and $\mathbf{v}_2$. At the initial moment, their position vectors are $\mathbf{r}_1$ and $\mathbf{r}_2$. What's the relationship between these four vectors for the particles to collide?

Conceptual Explanation

For two particles to collide, they must arrive at the same point in space **at the same time**. We can express this using kinematics. The position of a particle at any time $t$ is given by its initial position plus its displacement.

For a particle moving with a constant velocity, its position vector at time $t$ is: $$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_{\text{initial}} + \mathbf{v}t$$ For the particles to collide, their positions must be equal at some time $t$: $$\mathbf{r}_1 + \mathbf{v}_1 t = \mathbf{r}_2 + \mathbf{v}_2 t$$

Derivation and Collision Condition

By rearranging the fundamental collision equation, we can solve for the time of collision, $t$: $$\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2 = (\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1) t$$ Taking the magnitude of both sides and solving for $t$, we get the time it takes for the collision to occur: $$t = \frac{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|}{|\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1|}$$ The required relationship for a collision is that the **relative initial position vector** $(\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2)$ must be parallel to the **relative velocity vector** $(\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1)$. This is expressed by the equality of their unit vectors: $$\frac{\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|} = \frac{\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1}{|\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1|}$$
