IPhO 1967 Experimental Problem

Specific Heat of Petroleum

Problem Statement

Devices and materials given:

  1. Balance (without weights)
  2. Calorimeter
  3. Thermometer
  4. Source of voltage
  5. Switches
  6. Wires
  7. Electric heater
  8. Stop-watch
  9. Beakers
  10. Water
  11. Petroleum
  12. Sand (for balancing)

The task: Determine the specific heat of petroleum. The specific heat of water is \(1~cal/(g\cdot^\circ C)\) and the specific heat of the calorimeter is \(0.092~cal/(g\cdot^\circ C)\). Discuss the assumptions made in the solution.

Solution Methods

The provided devices allowed for several methods. Students used the following three main approaches to solve the problem:

1. Comparison of Velocity of Warming Up

This method compares the time it takes for water and petroleum to warm up by the same amount using a constant electric heater.

Heat absorbed by water and calorimeter:

\[ Q_{w}=m_{w}c_{w}\Delta t_{w}+m_{c}c_{c}\Delta t_{w} = (m_w c_w + m_c c_c)\Delta t_w \]

Heat absorbed by petroleum and calorimeter:

\[ Q_{p}=m_{p}c_{p}\Delta t_{p}+m_{c}c_{c}\Delta t_{p} = (m_p c_p + m_c c_c)\Delta t_p \]

Heat provided by the heater:

\[ Q_{heater}=P\tau = A\frac{U^{2}}{R}\tau \]

For the same temperature change (\(\Delta t_w = \Delta t_p = \Delta t\)) and assuming heat losses are negligible, we have:

\[ A\frac{U^{2}}{R}\tau_{w}=(m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t \]

\[ A\frac{U^{2}}{R}\tau_{p}=(m_{p}c_{p}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t \]

By taking the ratio of these two equations, the common terms cancel out:

\[ \frac{\tau_{w}}{\tau_{p}} = \frac{m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c}}{m_{p}c_{p}+m_{c}c_{c}} \]

With the simplification of using sand to make the masses equal (\(m_{w}=m_{p}=m_{c}=m\)), this simplifies to:

\[ \frac{\tau_{w}}{\tau_{p}} = \frac{c_{w}+c_{c}}{c_{p}+c_{c}} \]

Solving for \(c_{p}\):

\[ c_{p} = \left(\frac{\tau_{p}}{\tau_{w}}\right)(c_{w}+c_{c})-c_{c} \]

2. Comparison of Velocity of Cooling Down

This method is based on Newton's law of cooling. It involves heating the liquids first and then observing their cooling over time.

Heat lost by water and calorimeter:

\[ \Delta Q_{w}=(m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t_{w} \]

Heat lost by petroleum and calorimeter:

\[ \Delta Q_{p}=(m_{p}c_{p}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t_{p} \]

According to the solution, the ratio of heat lost is equal to the ratio of the cooling times:

\[ \frac{\Delta Q_{w}}{\Delta Q_{p}}=\frac{\tau_{w}}{\tau_{p}} \]

By combining the above equations and assuming the same temperature change for both liquids (\(\Delta t_w = \Delta t_p = \Delta t\)), we get:

\[ \frac{(m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t}{(m_{p}c_{p}+m_{c}c_{c})\Delta t} = \frac{\tau_{w}}{\tau_{p}} \]

Assuming \(m_{w}=m_{p}=m_{c}=m\), this simplifies to:

\[ \frac{c_{w}+c_{c}}{c_{p}+c_{c}} = \frac{\tau_{w}}{\tau_{p}} \]

Solving for \(c_{p}\):

\[ c_{p} = \left(\frac{\tau_{p}}{\tau_{w}}\right)(c_{w}+c_{c})-c_{c} \]

3. Traditional Heat Balance

This is a standard calorimetry method where petroleum is added to water in the calorimeter, and the final equilibrium temperature is measured.

Heat lost by hot water and calorimeter:

\[ Q_{lost}=(m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c})(t_{1}-t) \]

Heat gained by cold petroleum:

\[ Q_{gained}=m_{p}c_{p}(t-t_{2}) \]

By setting the heat lost equal to the heat gained (\(Q_{lost}=Q_{gained}\)), assuming no heat losses to the surroundings:

\[ (m_{w}c_{w}+m_{c}c_{c})(t_{1}-t)=m_{p}c_{p}(t-t_{2}) \]

With the simplification of using sand to make the masses equal (\(m_{w}=m_{p}=m_{c}=m\)), this simplifies to:

\[ (c_{w}+c_{c})(t_{1}-t)=c_{p}(t-t_{2}) \]

Solving for \(c_{p}\):

\[ c_{p}=(c_{w}+c_{c})\frac{t_{1}-t}{t-t_{2}} \]
